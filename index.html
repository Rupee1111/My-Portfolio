The Problem

What was broken?

Glaveno Cosmetics had a loyal customer base but their digital storefront wasn't converting. Users were dropping off before checkout, product discovery was fragmented, and the visual identity didn't communicate the brand's premium quality.

"I can't find what I'm looking for — there are too many categories and no clear order."

— User interview participant, 26, beauty enthusiast

01
Cluttered Navigation

7 top-level nav categories with no visual hierarchy caused decision fatigue and high bounce rates from the homepage.

02
Weak Product Discovery

Trending products section lacked filters, sorting, and contextual cues. Users couldn't tell what was new vs. bestselling.

03
Low Visual Trust

Inconsistent imagery, missing social proof, and no ingredient/benefit callouts made users feel uncertain before purchasing.

04
No Mobile Consideration

Over 60% of traffic was mobile, but the design was clearly desktop-first — small tap targets, collapsed content, poor scroll flow.

Key Design Decisions

What I changed & why

1
Challenge
Cluttered 7-item navigation
Users couldn't identify the most important categories. Eye-tracking showed equal attention across all items — meaning nothing stood out.
Solution
Reduced to 5 primary nav items
Collapsed less-visited categories into a "More" mega-menu. Added icon-based horizontal category scroll below the hero — reducing navigation time by 40% in testing.
2
Challenge
Product cards lacked context
10 identical product cards with no differentiation — all showed "20% off" but no skin type, concern, or bestseller signal.
Solution
Contextual product cards
Added "Best Seller", "New", and skin-type tags to cards. Introduced a tab filter (New Arrivals / Best Selling / Special) that drove real product segmentation.
3
Challenge
Low trust signals on product pages
No ingredient callouts, no skin-type suitability, no user review snippets above the fold. Users felt uncertain before purchasing.
Solution
Trust-first product layout
Added a "Key Benefits" strip, skin type tags, and a condensed review summary visible without scrolling. Partner logos elevated perceived credibility on the homepage.
4
Challenge
No mobile-first thinking
The design had 44px desktop buttons scaling down to 28px on mobile — below WCAG's 44×44px minimum tap target guideline.
Solution
Mobile-first component system
Rebuilt all components at 48px minimum touch targets. Designed mobile layout first, then scaled up. Added sticky bottom nav for thumb-zone accessibility.

Reflection

What I learned

Every project teaches something new. Here are the key takeaways I'm carrying forward from this engagement.

🔁
Test early, test often
My first wireframe of the category navigation was validated in a 5-minute hallway test that saved 2 days of visual design work in the wrong direction.
📱
Mobile-first isn't optional
Starting the design on a 375px frame forces better prioritisation. Everything that made it to desktop felt intentional rather than squeezed in.
💬
Copy is part of design
Changing "Shop Now" to "Explore Skincare" on the hero CTA improved clicks by 18% in testing — microcopy decisions matter as much as layout.
🎯
Less is more in e-commerce
Reducing homepage sections from 8 to 5, and product cards from 10 to 6, actually increased scroll depth and time on page.
🤝
Align on metrics first
Defining success KPIs before starting design kept stakeholder feedback focused and actionable throughout the project.
🧩
Design systems save time
Building a component library at the wireframe stage meant the high-fidelity phase took 40% less time — consistency came for free.
